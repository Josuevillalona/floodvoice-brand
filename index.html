Phase Color System

Same palette, shifted weight. FloodVoice never changes its colors between phases — it changes which color leads.

Phase 01
Preparedness
Terracotta leads. Community-feeling, warm, exploratory. Builds trust capital before the crisis.
"Ready, together, your neighborhood, your plan."
Phase 02
Active Flooding
Teal leads. Calm authority. Amber-gold for alerts — never red. Maximum clarity, minimum friction.
"Now, here, safe, confirmed, your zone."
Phase 03
Recovery
Terracotta returns. Warm, grounded, forward-looking. Where FloodVoice differentiates most from government tools.
"Coming back, here for you, together."

05 — Accessibility

Accessibility as Brand

Accessibility isn't compliance. It's the single most powerful trust signal for communities with historical reasons to be skeptical of government-adjacent tools.

Language First
Language selection at onboarding — no English default assumption. Spanish, Haitian Creole, Bengali, Chinese, Arabic, French, Korean, Russian as initial priorities.
Icon + Text Always
No icon-only navigation. Every interactive element carries both a visual and a text label — for low-literacy, non-English-speaking, and screen-reader users.
48px Touch Targets
Minimum 48px touch targets across the entire interface by default — not in an "accessibility mode." Elderly users are primary users, not edge cases.
4.5:1 Contrast
Teal on warm off-white passes WCAG AA while remaining warm. Contrast and warmth are not in conflict when the palette is designed with both requirements.
Aa 5.2:1
Aa 7.1:1
Multi-Modal Alerts
Active flooding triggers push + SMS + audio + vibration by default — not behind an accessibility setting. A resident without glasses benefits too.
Offline-First Design
Default to minimum-bandwidth rendering. NYC flooding consistently degrades cellular infrastructure. Image loading is opt-in after connectivity is confirmed.
06 — Do & Don't

What FloodVoice Looks Like.
What It Never Does.

The entire sector defaults to a narrow band of government blues, institutional authority signals, and clinical urgency cues. FloodVoice breaks from all of them.

Do
Deep Teal as primary Water-adjacent without government blue. Trust-signaling and culturally cross-resonant across the communities FloodVoice serves.
Amber-Gold for alerts Heightened attention without triggering fear-response. Distinct from every alert convention in this space.
Real community portraiture Named residents, CBO workers, neighborhood landmarks — with names and neighborhoods always shown.
Concentric rings as the mark Water, community gathering, alert broadcast, and resilience — all in one unclaimed visual metaphor.
Phase weight-shifting Same palette across all three phases — only the dominant color shifts. Brand recognition stays consistent through the crisis.
Never
Government blue (#103FEF) or emergency red Every government tool uses blue. Every emergency tool uses red. Both signal "not built for us" to communities with historical distrust of institutions.
Shield emblems or warning triangles Shield iconography carries law-enforcement connotations for immigrant communities. Triangles and exclamation marks are invisible from overuse.
Stock photography of "diverse families with emergency kits" Instantly recognizable as non-authentic. Appears across FEMA, Ready.gov, and virtually every civic tech marketing site.
Alert-first UI hierarchy Leading with emergency warnings trains users to associate the platform with stress rather than preparedness and community strength.
Pure white or pure black Pure white reads as clinical. Pure black reads as authoritarian. Neither belongs in a brand built on warmth and belonging.
